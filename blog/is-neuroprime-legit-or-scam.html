Defining a Supplement "Scam"

In the digital health space, a scam is rarely a company taking your money and sending nothing. Modern scams operate in the gray areas of commerce. They use auto-billing software to lock you into $90 monthly subscriptions that are impossible to cancel. They use proprietary blends to hide the fact that their capsules contain mostly rice flour. They utilize fake celebrity endorsements to manufacture authority.

A legitimate company, conversely, charges you once for exactly what you ordered. They disclose their ingredients. They utilize third-party merchant processors like Digistore24 to ensure your credit card data is secure and that refund policies are enforced by an impartial mediator.

The Danger of Unrealistic Expectations

A significant portion of consumer complaints labeling supplements as "scams" stems from profoundly misunderstood expectations. If a 45-year-old man purchases NeuroPrime expecting it to feel like a prescription pharmaceutical or a massive dose of synthetic caffeine, he will inevitably feel cheated when the results are subtle.

Nootropic supplements are not designed to override severe sleep deprivation or a terrible diet. If you attempt to use this product to compensate for four hours of sleep and chronic stress, it will fail, leading you to believe the product is fraudulent. The reality is that the product is a nutritional tool, not a miracle drug. Expecting a miracle from a dietary supplement is a setup for disappointment.
